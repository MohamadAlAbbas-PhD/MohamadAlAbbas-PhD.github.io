About Me

I am a PhD candidate in Demography and Sociology whose work sits at the intersection of planetary health, environmental demography, and population well-being.

My background spans Electrical and Computer Engineering and International Affairs, training that I bring to research questions requiring both technical rigor and policy relevance. Methodologically, I work at the intersection of spatial analytics, causal inference, and applied public policy, building reproducible pipelines that link remotely sensed climate surfaces (precipitation and temperature anomalies) with survey and administrative microdata.

Substantively, I study how environmental shocks translate into unequal health and well-being outcomes, with particular attention to children under five and pregnant women, and to the social stratification that shapes exposure, disruption, and recovery. A recurring theme across my projects is that administrative boundaries often obscure the ecological and climatic processes that drive risk in low- and middle-income settings; I therefore emphasize exposure definitions grounded in environmental context rather than political borders.

I was born in Beirut, Lebanon, an origin that continues to shape how I think about inequality, risk, and the ways institutions and infrastructure structure everyday life. My academic path has been deliberately cumulative: from engineering to international affairs to demography, statistics, and urban spatial analytics.

Research Themes

Empirical work at the intersection of climate, health, and social stratification, built for reproducibility and policy legibility.

Planetary Health

Examining how the degradation of natural systems, from deforestation and fossil fuel extraction to ocean warming, produces measurable harm to human populations, with a focus on the most vulnerable groups in low- and middle-income settings.

Environmental Demography

Linking acute and cumulative environmental hazards, including forest fires, oil spills, floods, and air pollution, to population-level health and mortality outcomes. I pay particular attention to how social stratification shapes who is exposed, who recovers, and who bears lasting consequences.

Climate-Health Linkages

Multi-country analysis of how precipitation and temperature anomalies stratify child nutrition, diarrheal disease, and respiratory outcomes across biomes. I use DHS and MICS microdata linked to CHIRPS and NOAA raster surfaces, with a core argument that biome context outperforms administrative fixed effects as a control strategy.

Spatial Analytics

Building reproducible geospatial pipelines that extract remotely sensed climate surfaces, apply vector and raster operations, and integrate satellite data with geocoded survey microdata. Tools include R, ArcGIS Pro, CHIRPS, NOAA grids, and NetCDF workflows designed for replication and scale.
